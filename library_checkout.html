Redesign the library self-checkout interface

We interact with electronic kiosks everyday - whether it’s getting cash from an ATM or using the self-checkout aisle at the grocery store.

I wanted to learn more about these interactions so I went to my local public library to observe people using the self-checkout kiosks.

separation of concerns

During the checkout process, the user asks two questions:

  1. Did I scan the last item correctly?
  2. Did I scan all my items?

The original design attempted to answer these two questions with one “list” layout. By dividing the layout into two sections, each section can serve a unique and separate purpose.

The list (left) provides an overview of all checked out items. The last checked out item is displayed as a large image (right).

By visually separating the last item from the entire list, users can easily and quickly determine the last item that was scanned.

image-based feedback

Whereas the original design was text based, the new layout incorporates both text and images. This hybrid model provides the following advantages:

Users strongly associate certain mediums (CDs, DVDs) with their cover-art. In those cases, an image will be a more memorable and effective representation than a title.

Images are unique in terms of color and shape whereas text is the same color, style and size. Therefore images are often more easily scannable.
